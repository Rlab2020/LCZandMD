# LCZandMD
Main codes for “Disparities in Mental Disorder Risk Across Residential Local Climate Zones in Middle-Aged and Older Urban Adults”

## Research Overview
Primary Exposure: Urban morphology characterized via the Local Climate Zone (LCZ) framework, evaluated at multiple granularities (including broad built vs. natural categories, structural spatial density types, and individual distinct LCZ profiles).

## Outcomes
Primary: Longitudinal incidence of clinical mental disorders (including depressive, anxiety, and substance use disorders).
Secondary: Subclinical psychiatric symptom dimensions (subjective well-being, PHQ-9 depressive symptoms, and GAD-7 anxiety symptoms) alongside structural neuroimaging phenotypes (scaled gray matter volumes).

## Workflow
We used a prospective urban cohort restricted to participants free of baseline psychiatric disorders with complete follow-up and exposure data. Baseline characteristics were summarized across environmental classifications, and cumulative person-years and sex-stratified incidence rates (per 100,000 person-years) were calculated for each exposure group.

Associations between built/natural environments and incident psychiatric disorders were assessed using Cox proportional hazards models and complementary Weibull-based Accelerated Failure Time (AFT) models, allowing estimation of both relative risk and time-to-event acceleration or delay under multiple covariate adjustment tiers.

To explore subclinical and neurobiological correlates, cross-sectional analyses were conducted after outlier exclusion. Multivariable logistic regression was used for psychological symptom burden, and linear regression for regional brain structure (gray and white matter ROIs). Sex- and age-stratified interaction analyses were performed with false discovery rate (FDR) correction.

Finally, counterfactual-based causal mediation analyses were applied to quantify the mediating roles of greenness (NDVI), land surface temperature (LST), and air pollution in linking urban form to psychological and neurobiological outcomes
